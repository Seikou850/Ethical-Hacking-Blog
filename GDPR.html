What is GDPR anyways?

GDPR is something that's thrown out a lot these days, especially in online spaces. You may know the term GDPR because it's one of the major factors in why those cookie menus appear every time you visit a new website. It's also the reason as to why if you have an account on a website or application, you can request all the data associated with the account and delete relevant data, whether it is just a portion of data or everything.

GDPR, or the General Data Protection Regulation, was something that is put into place by the European Union to protect the privacy and personal information of the citizens in the EU. The regulation was implemented in May of 2018 and has since become the gold standard in terms of data privacy. GDPR is something that should apply everywhere regardless of where you are, as long you are an EU citizen.

In short, it's made to protect the privacy of you and I everywhere online.

What does GDPR consider personal data?

The GDPR definition for personal data is very broad. The very open definition is written in a certain way so that the GDPR can cover all forms of personal information, regardless of what they are and the time they are made.


The image above shows how the GDPR defines personal data. As mentioned before, the definition is incredibly non-specific, meaning that every piece of personal data that you process or store will likely to fall under the definition. Some examples of what is considered personal data is shown as a baseline, but the way the definition is written definitely goes beyond the examples. Something that many may not think of is pseudonymous data. Pseudonymous data may also apply if it is easy to identify someone with.
This broad definition is honed in on a little further for 3 specific types of data. Genetic data, biometric data and data concerning health. The expanded definition is used to further future proof GDPR a little more.


Why is this relevant to sensitive data exposure?

Data exposure is rarely ever the fault of the user. Much of sensitive data exposure is caused due to hacking into databases. GDPR is in place to protect the users, and punish those who don't protect sensitive data properly. The regulation states that an organisation 'by design and default' consider data protection principles when designing any new product or activity.

GDPR is a gold standard in data privacy meaning it also deals with how personal data is processed. The general rule of thumb is that personal data must never be stored unless it is for legitimate purposes. The processor must then explicitly state what the purpose of storing the data and what they are doing with the data. The data subject must then agree to all these terms.

Business who handle personal data must take extreme care to ensure that they follow GDPR as punishment for mishandling data, whether it be accidental (personal data leak or hacking) or on purpose (storing data without consent to use for unethical practices) can be very high, depending on how serious the violation is. People affected by the mishandling of personal data also have the right to claim compensation as well.

All of this also could apply to an organisation outside the European Union as well. If the organisation is based out side the EU but does process data on people within the EU, a representative of your organisation must be appointed in an EU state which can communicate with data protection authorities within the EU. The organisation could also use a third party service to process the data on thier behalf, but they must be aware that the service they choose must be reliable and has sufficient data protection guarantees.

What should happen after sensitive data exposure has happened?

When a data breach has occured in an organisation, the organisation must notify all involved data subjects within 72 hours or they must deal with immediate penalties. However, if appropriate safeguards are put into place which can render the data unreadable, the organisation doesn't need to notify the involved parties.

As mentioned before, there are punishments for organisations which do have a data breach of some kind. There are 2 tiers of fines which can be issues to an organisation. Tier 1 is for less severe infringements while tier 2 infringements are for the more serious violations of GDPR. The fines are issued by the data regulator in the country that the organisation is based in. The data regualtor will decide which tier of fine you will be issued based on 10 criteria.


These 10 criteria and then evaluated against several of the GDPR articles, and depending on how many articles you have violated as well as the severity, fines will then be issued.
Tier 1 fines can be up to €10 million, or 2% of the firm's worldwide annual revenue from the preceding financial year, whichever amount is higher. Tier 2 fines are double this, a fine of up to €20 million or 4% of the firm's worldwide annual revenue from the preceding financial year, whichever amount is higher.
